Signal flow

One idea explains the whole machine: sound moves left to right through three stages, while the modulators underneath reach up and turn the knobs for you. Click a block to jump to its explanation.

The panel, section by section
Sound source

Digital oscillator

Type picks the engine (wavetable, FM, granular, virtual analog…); Wave, Timbre and Shape morph its character — their exact job changes per engine. Glide makes notes slide into each other. This section decides whether you get a soft flute or a screaming FM lead.

Tone shaping

Analog filter

Cutoff darkens or brightens the tone; Resonance adds a whistling peak at the cutoff point. Type switches LPF (keep lows — the classic), BPF (keep a middle slice — nasal), HPF (keep highs — thin and airy). Sweeping cutoff is the "wah" of all synth music.

Time shaping

Envelope

Shapes how each note evolves: Attack (fade-in time), Decay/Rel (fall time), Sustain (held level). Slow attack = pad, instant attack + short decay = pluck. Filter Amt lets the envelope punch the filter open on every note. Amp Mod ON makes it control volume — leave it on.

Time shaping

Cycling envelope

A second envelope with a trick: Mode sets it to Env (normal), Run (one-shot) or Loop — where it repeats forever like a fancy LFO. Route it in the matrix for rhythmic pulsing, tremolo, or textures that evolve on their own. Rise/Fall set its timing.

Movement

LFO

A wave too slow to hear, used to wiggle other knobs. Routed to pitch = vibrato; to cutoff = wobble; to volume = tremolo. Shape picks the wiggle pattern (triangle is smooth, square gives trills, random gets glitchy), Rate sets the speed, Sync locks it to tempo.

Movement

Mod matrix

The patch bay — the MicroFreak's superpower. Turn the Matrix knob to an intersection of a source row (LFO, envelopes, Pressure, Key/Arp) and a destination column (Pitch, Wave, Timbre, Cutoff + 3 assignable), click, and dial in an amount. This is where static sounds come alive.

Performance

Arp / Seq + Spice & Dice

The arpeggiator plays held chords as note patterns (Up, Order, Random, Pattern); the sequencer records looping phrases. Dice reshuffles your sequence's rhythm, Spice sets how much variation is applied — controlled happy accidents.

Performance

Touch keyboard

The flat capacitive keys sense pressure — press harder while holding a note and, via the matrix, that pressure can bend anything (brightness, vibrato depth, wave shape). Paraphonic mode allows 4 notes at once, sharing the single analog filter.

Three patch recipes

Knob positions are given as clock positions: 7 o'clock = fully left (minimum), 12 = middle, 5 o'clock = fully right (maximum). Start each patch from an initialized preset (Utility → Preset → Init). And remember: if a patch is silent, check Amp Mod is ON.

Tutorials & examples

Seven hands-on exercises, easiest first. Each takes 5–20 minutes at the synth. Do them in order — every one builds on the vocabulary of the previous. Click a tutorial to expand it.

T-01Meet the engines — your first sound from scratch5 min · easiest

Goal: understand that Type changes everything, and Wave/Timbre/Shape mean something different per engine.

  1. Initialize a patch: Utility → Preset → Init, confirm. You now have a plain, honest starting point.
  2. Hold a key and slowly turn Type through every engine. Listen for the big families: smooth (Vanalog, Waveshaper), metallic (Two Op. FM), vocal (Speech, Vowel), textural (Noise, Granular-style engines).
  3. Pick one engine you like. Now hold a key and sweep Wave fully left to right. Then Timbre. Then Shape.
  4. Repeat step 3 on a totally different engine — notice the same three knobs now do completely different things. That's by design: they're macro controls, not fixed parameters.

What to listen for: the moment a knob stops being "some knob" and becomes "the knob that adds growl on this engine." That mapping between hand and ear is the whole skill.

T-02The filter is an instrument — cutoff & resonance5 min

Goal: internalize what cutoff and resonance do before you ever route modulation to them.

  1. From an Init patch, set Type to Vanalog and Wave toward saw (bright = lots of harmonics = lots for the filter to eat).
  2. Hold a low note. Sweep Cutoff from fully open down to closed and back. This is "the wah."
  3. Set Cutoff to 12 o'clock. Now raise Resonance step by step while re-sweeping Cutoff. Hear the whistle appear at the moving cutoff point.
  4. Push Resonance near max and sweep slowly — the filter almost sings on its own. Pull it back to taste.
  5. Now press the filter Type button: try the same sweep with HPF (sound gets thin from below) and BPF (nasal, radio-like).

What to listen for: resonance doesn't add anything new — it emphasizes what's already at the cutoff. That's why it "squelches" when the cutoff moves.

T-03First matrix routing — give yourself vibrato10 min · the big one

Goal: make your first source → destination connection. Once this clicks, the whole matrix is yours.

  1. Start from any sound you like. Set the LFO to triangle shape, Rate around 9–10 o'clock (a gentle wobble speed).
  2. Turn the Matrix knob until the cursor sits on the intersection of row LFO and column Pitch.
  3. Click the Matrix knob to select it, then turn to dial in a small amount, around +5 to +10. Hold a note: vibrato.
  4. Too seasick? Reduce the amount. Too slow? Raise LFO Rate. Amount = depth, Rate = speed.
  5. Now move the cursor to LFO → Cutoff instead, amount ~+20, and slow the Rate way down: the tone breathes. Congratulations — you just built the pad trick from recipe 02.
  6. Undo a routing anytime: select the intersection, click, dial the amount back to 0.

What to listen for: the same LFO, routed to two different destinations, makes two totally different musical effects. Modulation = source × destination × amount. That's the formula for everything.

T-04Play with pressure — expressive touch keys10 min

Goal: use the touch keyboard's pressure sensing so the sound responds to how hard you press.

  1. Load your pad from recipe 02, or any sustained sound.
  2. In the matrix, route Press → Cutoff, amount around +25.
  3. Hold a chord lightly, then slowly press harder into the keys. The sound blooms open under your fingers.
  4. Add a second routing: Press → Assign 1. To assign the destination, hold the Assign 1 button and wiggle the knob you want (try Wave). Now pressure morphs the oscillator too.
  5. Try a negative amount on Press → Cutoff instead: pressing harder now darkens the sound — equally musical, opposite mood.

What to listen for: the difference between a keyboard that plays notes and one that plays gestures. This is the MicroFreak's most underrated feature.

T-05Self-playing machine — arp, sequencer, Spice & Dice15 min · most fun

Goal: build a groove that plays itself and mutates while you sculpt the sound with both hands.

  1. Load your bass stab from recipe 03. Press Arp and hold a 3–4 note chord: instant pattern. Try the Up / Order / Random / Pattern modes and Oct range 2.
  2. Set Rate to a comfortable tempo (or Sync if you're clocking to other gear). Hold Shift + Rate for Swing — around 55–60% adds groove.
  3. Press Hold so the arp keeps running hands-free.
  4. Now the magic row: tap Dice to reshuffle the rhythm, and slide Spice up to decide how wild the variations get. Low Spice = subtle mutations, high Spice = barely your pattern anymore.
  5. While it runs, perform with Cutoff, Resonance, and the Bend strip. Two hands, zero notes played, full track energy.
  6. Bonus: switch from Arp to Seq (Shift + Arp|Seq), record a short phrase step by step, and do the same Spice & Dice treatment to a melody you wrote.

What to listen for: when a Dice roll surprises you with something better than what you programmed — save it. Half of electronic music is curated accidents.

T-06Build your own drum kit — kick, snare, hat20 min · sound design

Goal: design three drum sounds from scratch and save them as presets. One honest thing first: the MicroFreak plays one preset at a time, so a "kit" here means three saved patches — we'll deal with playing them together in T-07. Drum design is the best envelope workout there is: drums are nothing but envelopes.

  1. Kick. Init patch → Type Vanalog, Wave fully left (toward sine — pure low tone). Amp Mod ON. Attack min, Sustain zero, Decay/Rel ~9 o'clock. Octave down twice. Now the secret ingredient: set the Cycling Envelope to Env mode, Rise minimum, Fall ~9 o'clock, and in the matrix route CycEnv → Pitch with a big positive amount (~+40). Each note now starts high and drops fast — that pitch-drop is the "boom." Tune the drop speed with Fall.
  2. Tap a low key. Too clicky? Slightly raise Attack. Too boomy? Shorten Decay. Save it: hold Save, pick a slot, name it KICK.
  3. Snare. Init → Type Noise (the engine that blends noise with tone — turn Wave to set the mix, roughly half noise / half tone). Filter BPF, Cutoff ~1 o'clock, Resonance ~12. Attack min, Sustain zero, Decay/Rel ~9–10 o'clock. Route CycEnv → Pitch again but a smaller amount (~+15) for the snap. Save as SNARE.
  4. Hi-hat. Init → Type Noise, Wave fully toward pure noise. Filter HPF, Cutoff ~3 o'clock (only the sizzle survives). Attack min, Sustain zero, Decay/Rel almost minimum — a tick of air. Save as HAT. For an open hat, just lengthen Decay.
  5. Play each kit piece across the keyboard and notice pitch still matters: a kick on a lower key hits deeper; hats up high get thinner. Pick your favorite key per drum — that choice is part of the kit.

What to listen for: every drum is the same trick — noise or tone, a snappy amp envelope, and a fast pitch drop for punch. Once you hear that, you can "read" any drum machine sound by ear.

T-07Record a drum pattern and combine it with bass20 min · arrangement

Goal: sequence a beat, then get bass on top. Since the MicroFreak is one voice / one preset at a time, there are two paths: fake a whole kit inside one patch using the sequencer's automation lanes (pure MicroFreak), or record the drum loop and layer bass over it (the studio way). Do both.

  1. Path A — one-patch beat. Load your KICK from T-06. Enter the sequencer: Shift + Arp|Seq until Seq is active, pick sequence A or B.
  2. Record a pattern in step mode: press Record, then tap keys to fill steps — kick on your chosen low key on steps 1, 5, 9, 13 (a basic 4/4), and tap Rest (the dedicated silence key) for empty steps. A 16-step pattern is plenty.
  3. Now the trick that makes one patch sound like a kit: the sequencer has 4 modulation lanes that record knob motion per step. Start lane recording (hold Record while the sequence plays) and turn Cutoff + Wave only during the off-beat steps — closed/dark on kicks, bright/noisy on off-beats. Those bright noisy steps read as hats. One preset, but it grooves like a kit.
  4. Add Spice & Dice on top for variation, and swing (~57%) for pocket.
  5. Path B — real layering. Record 8 bars of your beat into anything that loops: your DAW, a phone recorder app, or a looper pedal. Even a rough phone recording works for jamming.
  6. While the loop plays back, switch the MicroFreak to your bass stab (recipe 03) and play or sequence a bassline over the drums. Classic move: bass notes on the off-beats between kicks, so kick and bass never fight for the low end.
  7. If you have a DAW: record the bass as a second track, then keep going — pads (recipe 02) as a third layer, and suddenly the one-voice MicroFreak has become a full track, one layer at a time. This overdub workflow is exactly how most MicroFreak tracks are made.

What to listen for: in Path A, how automation per step changes the identity of each hit without changing the preset. In Path B, the moment kick and bass lock — if the low end sounds muddy, shift bass notes off the kick steps or pull the bass Cutoff down slightly.

Arp & Seq — from 0 to 100

The complete path from "what does this button do" to running the MicroFreak as a self-playing groove machine. Work through the levels in order; each one assumes the previous. The core distinction to hold onto the whole way: the arpeggiator generates patterns from what you're holding right now, the sequencer replays what you recorded.

LVL 0–15Concepts & the buttonsarp vs seq

Goal: know exactly which mode you're in and what each control in the ARP/SEQ block does.

  1. The Arp | Seq button cycles the section: white label = Arp, blue label = Seq (blue text anywhere on the panel means "Shift function or second mode"). Off = plain keyboard.
  2. Arp: hold notes → the synth plays them one at a time in a pattern. Let go → it stops. Nothing is stored.
  3. Seq: a recorded phrase loops back exactly as you entered it, hands-free. Each preset stores two sequences, A and B — think verse and chorus.
  4. Rate/Swing sets the speed of both. Oct | Mod sets the arp's octave span (Arp mode) or selects modulation lanes (Seq mode) — same button, two lives.
  5. Hold latches arp notes so you can take your hands off; in Seq mode the same key becomes Tie/Rest for entering silences and held notes.

Checkpoint: you can switch between Arp, Seq, and off without thinking, and you know why your held chord stops when you release keys in Arp but the Seq keeps going.

LVL 15–35Arpeggiator basics — modes, octaves, hold10 min

Goal: get musical results from the four arp modes and understand why note order and octave range matter.

  1. Load a pluck or bass sound (recipes 01/03 — short envelopes make arps readable). Activate Arp, hold a simple triad (e.g. C–E–G).
  2. Cycle the modes: Up climbs low→high, repeat. Order plays notes in the order your fingers landed — re-press the chord with a different finger order and hear the melody change. Order is secretly a composition tool.
  3. Random picks from your held notes unpredictably — great for hats-like shimmer and IDM textures. Pattern generates a repeating random pattern from your notes; press Pattern again to roll a new one until you find a keeper.
  4. Press Oct to widen the range to 2, 3, 4 octaves. A 1-octave Up arp is a bassline; the same notes at 3 octaves is an arpeggio cascade. Same fingers, different music.
  5. Press Hold, release your hands. The arp keeps running. Now add or remove single notes while it plays — live chord editing. Press each held note again to remove it from the pattern.

Checkpoint: hold one chord and, using only mode + octave + Hold, produce four recognizably different musical parts without moving your left hand.

LVL 35–50Time feel — rate, sync, swing, gatethe groove level

Goal: control the rhythmic feel — the difference between a demo and a track.

  1. With an arp running, turn Rate freely. Unsynced, it's a smooth speed control — even musical as an effect (slow crawl to fast shimmer).
  2. Now enable Sync. Rate snaps to musical divisions (1/4, 1/8, 1/16, triplets…). This is what you want 95% of the time, and it's mandatory when playing with other gear or a DAW.
  3. Hold Shift and turn Rate: that's Swing, from 50% (robotic) up to 75% (drunk). The sweet spot for most grooves is 54–60% — every off-beat lands slightly late, and suddenly the pattern has a pocket.
  4. Find the gate length setting in Utility: short gates = staccato, ticking, techno; long gates = notes blur legato into each other. Same pattern, opposite energy.
  5. Clocking to the outside world: in Utility → MIDI, the MicroFreak can follow external clock (from a DAW or drum machine via USB/MIDI) or be the master. When following, Rate divisions stay locked to the incoming tempo.

Checkpoint: take one 1/16 arp and, changing only swing and gate length, make it sound like (a) precise techno, (b) lazy hip-hop, (c) flowing ambient. If you can hear those three, you own this level.

LVL 50–65Sequencer basics — recording A & B15 min

Goal: comfortably record phrases both step by step and live, with rests and ties, into both sequence slots.

  1. Switch to Seq (Shift + Arp|Seq), choose sequence A. Sequences can be up to 64 steps long.
  2. Step recording (the composer's way): with the sequencer stopped, press Record. Each key you tap fills the next step. Wrong note? Keep going — you can re-record; sketching fast beats fixing slow.
  3. Silence and length: tap Tie/Rest alone for an empty step (rest); hold a key and tap Tie/Rest to stretch that note across extra steps (tie). Rests are not gaps in your idea — they are the rhythm.
  4. Press Record again to exit, press play — your phrase loops. Now record something contrasting into sequence B and practice switching A↔B in time: instant song sections.
  5. Real-time recording (the performer's way): start the sequence playing, press Record, and just play. Your timing is captured and quantized to the grid. Great for parts you can feel but can't spell out step by step.
  6. While a sequence loops, the keyboard stays live — play over your own loop. With Paraphonic on, you can hold pad notes on top of a running line.

Checkpoint: a 16-step bassline in A with at least four rests and one tied note, a variation in B, and you can flip between them without the loop stumbling.

LVL 65–80Modulation lanes — sequences that turn knobsthe deep feature

Goal: use the 4 modulation lanes so the sequence doesn't just play notes — it performs the knobs too. This is what you already touched in T-07; now make it systematic.

  1. With a sequence playing, press Record and turn a knob — say Cutoff. The motion is captured per step into a mod lane. Stop recording; the sequence now replays your filter moves forever.
  2. The Oct | Mod button (Seq mode) selects between the 4 lanes — the LEDs show which are in use. Four lanes = four different knobs automated at once: e.g. Cutoff + Wave + Resonance + Glide.
  3. Per-step editing: while step-recording a lane, you can set a value for each step individually — hold the step's position and set the knob. Precise, plotted automation instead of a recorded gesture.
  4. Musical recipes to try: Cutoff lane opening every 4th step (pumping); Wave lane morphing across 16 steps (evolving timbre); Glide lane raised on just two steps (acid slides on those notes only); Resonance lane spiking on the last step (a squelch that announces the loop).
  5. To clear a lane you don't like, re-record over it, or hold the lane and clear it — a wrong automation is never a commitment.

Checkpoint: a static 8-note sequence that, through mod lanes alone, evolves so much over the loop that a listener would swear you're performing it live.

LVL 80–90Spice, Dice & performance movescontrolled chaos

Goal: use randomness as an instrument, and perform a running pattern like a DJ performs a track.

  1. With any arp or sequence running: tap Dice — it rerolls the pattern's rhythmic DNA (gates, accents, timing variations). Spice sets how much of that reroll is applied: low = seasoning, high = mutation.
  2. Performance loop: Dice → listen 2 bars → keep or Dice again. When a roll is magic, lower Spice to lock the vibe in and keep playing. Randomness proposes; you dispose.
  3. Layer the physical controls: while the pattern runs, ride Cutoff with one hand and the Bend strip with the other. Tap Oct arrows to throw the whole pattern up or down an octave for instant "drop" moments.
  4. Structure a live 4-minute jam: A-sequence 8 bars → raise Spice gradually → Dice at the transition → switch to B → pull Spice back. That arc — order, chaos, new order — is a complete piece of music.
  5. Hear something better than what you wrote? Save immediately (hold Save). The preset stores its sequences, so a lucky Dice roll can become tomorrow's starting point.

Checkpoint: record (on your phone, even) a 3-minute jam from one preset that has a beginning, a build, a turn, and an ending — no hands leaving the synth.

LVL 90–100Expert corner — the seq as a modulation sourcefull circle

Goal: close the loop with the mod matrix — the arp/seq isn't just a note player, it's a modulation source, and that changes everything.

  1. Look at the matrix source rows: the bottom one is Key/Arp. It outputs the pitch of whatever the keyboard, arp, or sequencer is currently playing — as a modulation signal.
  2. Route Key/Arp → Cutoff (moderate positive amount): now higher notes are automatically brighter — keyboard tracking, like acoustic instruments behave. Your arps instantly sound more organic.
  3. Route Key/Arp → Wave or → Timbre: every pitch in the sequence gets its own oscillator character. A single 8-note loop becomes 8 different voices taking turns.
  4. Now stack the whole tower: sequence plays notes → mod lanes ride four knobs → Key/Arp routing colors each pitch → looping Cycling Envelope pulses the volume → LFO breathes the filter → Spice & Dice mutate the rhythm. Six layers of automatic motion, one preset, zero hands. This — machines modulating machines — is the "freak" in MicroFreak.
  5. Final exam: build a patch that plays itself for five minutes and stays interesting. Then delete one modulation layer at a time and listen to what disappears — the fastest ear-training for what each layer contributes.

Checkpoint: you no longer think "arp section" and "matrix section" — you think in one system where notes, knobs, and randomness all modulate each other. That's 100.

Almost everything on a synth boils down to one sentence: an oscillator makes a tone, a filter darkens it, an envelope shapes it in time, and modulation moves any of those automatically. Every fancy term on this page is just a detail of one of those four jobs.

How it maps to the hardware

This toy is deliberately smaller than the real thing. Knowing exactly where the map ends is part of understanding the territory.

Same idea

Waveform ↔ Type + Wave

Four raw waveforms stand in for the MicroFreak's dozen-plus engines. The principle transfers exactly: more angular wave = more harmonics = brighter, and more for the filter to chew on. Saw is the greediest, sine has nothing but the fundamental.

Same idea

Cutoff / Resonance ↔ the analog filter

A real low-pass filter with a resonant peak, same as the hardware's LPF mode. There's also a small built-in filter envelope so notes have punch — that's the hardware's Filter Amt baked in at a fixed amount.

Same idea

A · D · S · R ↔ the envelope

Identical concept, and the visualization above draws the exact shape you've dialled in. The green band shows how far open the filter sits. Attack and Decay are time; Sustain is a level, not a time — the single most common beginner mix-up.

Missing here

What the real one has that this doesn't

No mod matrix, no LFO, no cycling envelope, no pressure sensing, no arp/seq, no Spice & Dice, no paraphony beyond simply holding several keys. Those are the parts you have to learn on the hardware — tabs 01's tutorials cover them.

Things to try right now

Five two-minute experiments. Each one isolates a single variable, which is the only reliable way to learn what a control actually does.

P-01Sustain is a level, not a time2 min

Set Attack 1, Decay 30, Release 20. Now hold a key down for a long time and change only Sustain.

  • Sustain 0 → the note dies while your finger is still down. That's a pluck.
  • Sustain 100 → the note never drops; Decay does nothing audible at all.
  • Sustain 50 → the note falls to half loudness and stays there until you let go.

Why it matters: Decay and Sustain only make sense together. Decay is how fast it falls, Sustain is how far it falls to. When Sustain is maxed, Decay is switched off in practice.

P-02Brightness lives in two places2 min

Pick Sine and open Cutoff all the way. Almost nothing changes — a sine has no harmonics for the filter to remove. Now switch to Saw and repeat the same Cutoff sweep.

Why it matters: the filter can only take away. If a sound is dull with the filter wide open, the fix is at the oscillator, not the filter. Half of "my patch sounds thin" problems are this.

P-03Resonance and the edge of self-oscillation3 min

Saw wave, Cutoff around 40. Raise Resonance to 90 and then move Cutoff slowly up and down while holding a note.

Why it matters: the whistle you hear tracks the cutoff point, not the note. That's the acid squelch, the dubstep wobble, and the classic filter sweep — all the same phenomenon at different speeds.

P-04Build the three recipes by ear4 min

Without touching the preset buttons, try to hit each target from the recipes in tab 01, then press the preset to check yourself.

  • Pluck — Attack min, Sustain 0, Decay short, Cutoff high.
  • Pad — Attack high, Sustain high, Release long, Cutoff middling.
  • Bass stab — Attack min, Sustain medium, Release short, Cutoff high, Resonance up.

Why it matters: guessing before checking is what turns knowledge into reflex. Reading the answer first teaches you nothing.

P-05Set the voice for your theory practice1 min

Before heading to tab 03, dial in a voice that makes musical intervals easy to hear: Triangle or Sine, Attack ~10, Decay 40, Sustain 60, Release 30, Cutoff ~70, Resonance low.

Why it matters: a buzzy resonant saw has so many harmonics that two notes a fifth apart get muddled. Pure-ish tones make intervals and chord qualities obvious. Once your ear knows the interval, come back and make it nasty.

Theme 1 · Lessons 01–04

Foundations — pitch, distance, time

Before scales and chords there are only two raw materials: how high a note is, and when it happens. Get these four lessons into your ear and everything after them is bookkeeping.

L-01Notes, octaves and the twelve semitonesstart here

Goal: understand why the keyboard looks the way it does, and why music only ever needs twelve note names.

A note is a vibration at some speed, measured in hertz. Play 440 vibrations per second and you get the A above middle C. Double it to 880 and you get a note that sounds so similar we give it the same name — A again, one octave higher. That doubling relationship is the one piece of physics all music sits on, and it's why the keyboard pattern repeats.

Western music slices each octave into twelve equal steps, called semitones (or half steps). Twelve steps up from any note lands you back on the same note name, one octave up. Two semitones make a whole step. That's it — the entire pitch alphabet is twelve items long, repeated forever in both directions.

The white and black keys are just a visual aid for a scale we'll meet in lesson 05. Physically, every neighbouring key — white to black, black to white, or the two white pairs E–F and B–C — is exactly one semitone. C♯ and D♭ are the same key with two names; that's called enharmonic spelling, and which name you use depends on the key you're in.

Hear it

L-02Intervals — the distance between two notesthe core skill

Goal: learn the twelve interval names and start recognising them by ear. Everything later — scales, chords, harmony — is built out of these.

An interval is the distance between two notes, counted in semitones. Each distance has a name and, more importantly, a character: a perfect fifth sounds open and stable, a minor second sounds like a horror film. Musicians hear intervals rather than counting them, and that ear is learnable — it just takes repetition, which is what these buttons are for.

The naming looks odd at first because it comes from counting letter names rather than semitones. C up to E spans three letters (C-D-E), so it's a "third" — but it's a major third because it's four semitones. C up to E♭ also spans three letters, so it's still a third, but three semitones makes it a minor third. That major/minor distinction is the single most consequential fact in this whole guide: it's what makes a chord sound happy or sad.

Play two notes one after the other and you have a melodic interval. Play them together and it's a harmonic interval. Same distance, very different job.

L-03Rhythm — beat, bar, subdivision, groovethe other half

Goal: name the parts of time. Pitch gets all the attention, but rhythm is what makes people move.

The beat is the steady pulse you tap your foot to; its speed is tempo, measured in beats per minute. Beats group into bars (measures), and how many beats sit in a bar is the time signature. Four beats per bar — 4/4 — covers the overwhelming majority of pop, rock and electronic music. Three per bar is a waltz. The first beat of each bar is the downbeat, and it carries a natural accent whether you play one or not.

Between beats you get subdivisions: split each beat in two for eighth notes, in four for sixteenths, in three for triplets. A pattern's identity comes mostly from which subdivision it lives on. And when notes land deliberately off the strong beats, that's syncopation — the single most reliable way to make a straight pattern feel alive.

Then there's swing, which you already met on the MicroFreak's Rate knob: delay every second subdivision slightly and a mechanical grid starts to breathe. 50% is dead straight, 66% is a full triplet shuffle, and most good grooves live between 54% and 60%.

L-04Consonance, dissonance and the harmonic serieswhy any of it works

Goal: understand why some interval combinations sound stable and others sound like they need to go somewhere. This is the physics under all the rules that follow.

Any real instrument playing a note doesn't produce one frequency — it produces a fundamental plus a stack of quieter overtones above it, at exact whole-number multiples: 2×, 3×, 4×, 5× and so on. That stack is the harmonic series, and it is the ranking system music inherited. The 2× overtone is the octave. The 3× is the perfect fifth. The 5× is the major third. Those are precisely the intervals we find most consonant — because when two notes share overtones, our ears fuse them into one pleasant event.

Notes whose frequencies form simple ratios share lots of overtones and sound consonant: octave 2:1, fifth 3:2, fourth 4:3, major third 5:4. Notes with ugly ratios share almost none, and their close-but-not-equal overtones collide and produce audible beating — that's dissonance. The tritone, at roughly 45:32, is the classic offender.

The crucial reframing: dissonance is not a mistake. It is the fuel. A piece of music that is consonant throughout is wallpaper. Tension asking to be resolved is what gives music direction, and the entire craft of harmony — lessons 10 through 17 — is about deploying dissonance deliberately and then paying it off.

That final stack is a C dominant-ninth chord and you never chose it — it's simply what a single vibrating string already contains. Harmony was discovered, not invented.

Theme 2 · Lessons 05–09

Scales and keys — choosing your twelve down to seven

All twelve semitones sound like nothing in particular. Pick seven of them in a specific pattern and you get a home base, a hierarchy, and a mood. That selection is a scale, and committing to one is a key.

L-05The major scale — the ruler everything is measured againstthe big one

Goal: know the major scale as a pattern of steps, not as a set of white keys, so you can build it starting anywhere.

A scale is an ordered selection of notes from the twelve, used as the raw material for a piece. The major scale takes seven of them in one specific pattern of whole (W) and half (H) steps:

W – W – H – W – W – W – H

Start that pattern on C and it lands entirely on white keys — which is the only reason the piano looks the way it does. Start it on G and you need one black key (F♯) to keep the pattern intact. Start it on any of the twelve notes and you get that same bright, resolved major sound, just higher or lower. The pattern is the scale; the starting note is just where you put it.

The starting note is the tonic — home. Every other note is described by its number, its scale degree, and each degree has a job and a traditional name: 1 tonic, 2 supertonic, 3 mediant, 4 subdominant, 5 dominant, 6 submediant, 7 leading tone. You mostly need three of them. Degree 1 is home. Degree 5 is the strongest away-from-home. Degree 7, the leading tone, sits one semitone below home and pulls toward it so hard that it practically resolves itself.

L-06Minor scales — natural, harmonic and melodicthe other mood

Goal: build all three minor scales and understand why there are three rather than one.

The natural minor scale flattens degrees 3, 6 and 7 of the major. Its step pattern is W-H-W-W-H-W-W. The flattened third is what does the emotional work — remember from lesson 02 that major third versus minor third is the happy/sad switch. Flatten it and the whole scale turns.

Here is the trick that saves enormous effort: A natural minor uses exactly the same notes as C major, just starting from a different place. Every major scale has a relative minor three semitones below its tonic, sharing all seven notes. Same notes, different home, completely different feeling — proof that a key is about hierarchy, not just note content.

But natural minor has a weakness: its seventh degree is a whole step below the tonic, so it doesn't pull home the way a leading tone should. Composers fixed this by raising it, producing the harmonic minor — which restores the pull but leaves an exotic three-semitone gap between degrees 6 and 7. Fixing that gap by also raising the sixth on the way up gives the melodic minor, traditionally played raised ascending and natural descending. Three scales, one problem, two patches.

Distinguish relative from parallel: A minor is the relative minor of C major (same notes, different tonic). C minor is the parallel minor of C major (same tonic, different notes). Swapping between parallel keys mid-song is a favourite trick — see lesson 17.

The three minors, all on A

Harmonic minor's leap from F to G♯ is that three-semitone gap. It's why the scale sounds Middle-Eastern or Baroque depending on what you do with it — and why so much metal lives here.

L-07The modes — seven flavours from one set of notesthe fun one

Goal: stop finding modes mystical. They are one simple idea applied seven times.

You already know the idea from lesson 06: take C major's notes but treat A as home and you get a minor scale. Do that from every degree and you get the seven modes. Same seven notes, seven different homes, seven different characters — because the pattern of steps around the new tonic is different each time.

Playing them from the white keys makes the construction obvious but hides the character, because your ear keeps hearing C major. The genuinely useful way to learn modes is to build them all from the same tonic and compare — then each mode's one distinguishing note jumps out. That distinguishing note is called the characteristic note, and it's the whole personality of the mode:

  • Ionian — the major scale. The reference.
  • Dorian — minor with a raised 6th. Minor but hopeful. Funk, folk, Scarborough Fair.
  • Phrygian — minor with a ♭2. Spanish, sinister, metal.
  • Lydian — major with a ♯4. Floating, wondrous, film scores.
  • Mixolydian — major with a ♭7. Bluesy, rock, unresolved-in-a-good-way.
  • Aeolian — natural minor. The reference sad scale.
  • Locrian — ♭2 and ♭5. Unstable to the point of being nearly unusable. Include it for completeness.

L-08Pentatonic and blues scales — fewer notes, no wrong onesinstant results

Goal: get a scale you can improvise with immediately, and understand why removing notes makes things easier rather than more limited.

The major pentatonic is the major scale minus degrees 4 and 7 — five notes left. Those two are precisely the notes that create the strongest tension (remember the leading tone's pull and the 4→3 pull from lesson 05). Remove them and nothing you play can sound wrong, because no note is straining toward anywhere. That's why pentatonics turn up in folk music on every continent independently: they're the path of least resistance.

The minor pentatonic is the same five notes started from the relative minor — 1, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭7. This is the scale of essentially all rock guitar soloing. Add one more note, the ♭5 — the blue note — and you get the blues scale: six notes, and the added one is deliberately dissonant, used as a passing sting rather than a resting place.

L-09Keys, key signatures and the circle of fifthsthe map

Goal: see how the twelve keys relate to each other, so moving between them stops being guesswork.

A key is a commitment: this passage uses this scale, with this note as home. Since each major scale needs a specific set of sharps or flats to keep its W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern, each key has a key signature — the fixed list of accidentals that apply throughout.

Those signatures are not arbitrary. Go up a perfect fifth from C to G and the scale needs exactly one sharp. Up another fifth to D, two sharps. Another to A, three. Keep going and after twelve fifths you arrive back at C. Arrange the twelve keys in that order and you get the circle of fifths — the single most useful diagram in music.

It earns its keep three ways. Neighbouring keys share almost all their notes, so moving to an adjacent key (modulation) sounds smooth while jumping across the circle sounds jarring. Root motion by descending fifth is the strongest chord movement there is — it's why ii–V–I works, and why entire jazz tunes are just laps around the circle. And it tells you a key's relative minor at a glance, sitting on an inner ring.

Going the other direction from C, down a fifth to F, adds flats instead: F has one, B♭ two, E♭ three. Sharps and flats are the same circle approached from opposite sides, meeting at the bottom where F♯ and G♭ are the same key with two spellings.

Theme 3 · Lessons 10–14

Harmony — stacking notes and moving between them

Notes played together become chords; chords played in sequence become a story with tension and payoff. This theme is the engine room of songwriting, and the payoff for everything in themes 1 and 2.

L-10Triads — stacking thirdschords begin

Goal: build the four triad types from scratch and hear what each one is for.

A chord is three or more notes sounded together. The standard way to build one is to stack thirds: take a note, skip one scale step, take the next, skip again, take the next. Three notes — a triad — named root, third and fifth after their intervals from the root.

Which kind of thirds you stack determines everything. Recall from lesson 02 that a major third is four semitones and a minor third is three. Stack them in different orders and you get all four triads:

  • Major — major third then minor third (4 + 3). Bright, resolved.
  • Minor — minor third then major third (3 + 4). Dark, resolved.
  • Diminished — minor then minor (3 + 3). Tense, unstable, wants to move.
  • Augmented — major then major (4 + 4). Eerie, suspended, directionless.

Notice major and minor are the same two intervals in swapped order, and that both stable triads contain a perfect fifth between the outer notes. The two unstable ones don't — diminished has a tritone, augmented has a minor sixth. Stability is the perfect fifth, exactly as lesson 04 predicted.

Finally, a chord doesn't care which note is at the bottom. Move the root up an octave and you get the first inversion; move the third up too and you get the second inversion. Same chord, same name, different colour and different bass note. Which arrangement you choose is the voicing, and it matters enormously for how smooth a progression sounds — that's lesson 15.

L-11Diatonic chords and Roman numeralsthe toolkit

Goal: derive the seven chords that belong to a key, and learn the notation that makes progressions portable between keys.

Build a triad on each degree of the major scale, using only notes from that scale, and something remarkable falls out: the qualities are always in the same order, in every major key. Degrees 1, 4 and 5 give major chords. Degrees 2, 3 and 6 give minor chords. Degree 7 gives a diminished chord. Always. That fixed pattern is the reason a key feels like a coherent place.

Because the pattern is universal, we name the chords by Roman numeral instead of by letter: uppercase for major, lowercase for minor, a small circle for diminished. So every major key has I ii iii IV V vi vii°. Write a progression as I–V–vi–IV and it works in any key — you've described the relationships, which is what actually produces the sound, rather than the specific letters, which are just where you happened to put it.

These chords also have functions, which is the deeper organising idea. Tonic chords (I, vi, iii) feel like home. Subdominant chords (IV, ii) feel like departure. Dominant chords (V, vii°) feel like tension demanding resolution. The classic shape of a phrase is tonic → subdominant → dominant → tonic: leave home, get further away, build tension, come back.

Minor keys work the same way with a different pattern: i ii° III iv v VI VII. In practice the v is usually raised to a major V by borrowing harmonic minor's leading tone — without it, minor keys have no proper pull home.

L-12Seventh chords — adding colour and directionjazz starts here

Goal: stack one more third onto every triad and understand why the dominant seventh in particular changed music.

Keep stacking thirds past the fifth and you reach the seventh. A seventh chord has four notes and is dramatically more expressive than a triad, because the added note is a mild dissonance that gives the chord an opinion about where to go next.

Six types matter. Major 7th (major triad + major 7th) is lush and dreamy. Dominant 7th (major triad + minor 7th) is tense and directional. Minor 7th (minor triad + minor 7th) is smooth and mellow. Half-diminished (diminished triad + minor 7th) is plaintive. Diminished 7th (diminished + diminished 7th) is maximum instability. Minor-major 7th is the sound of a spy film.

The dominant seventh deserves special attention. Built on degree 5, it contains the tritone between degrees 4 and 7 of the key — and that tritone resolves outward to the tonic's third and root exactly as you heard in lesson 04. It is the strongest pull toward home in all of tonal music, which is why "dominant" means both "the chord on degree 5" and "the chord that demands resolution."

Applying the same stacking to every degree gives the diatonic sevenths: Imaj7 · ii7 · iii7 · IVmaj7 · V7 · vi7 · viiø7. Only one of the seven is a dominant seventh — the one on degree 5. That uniqueness is what makes V7 announce the key so unambiguously.

L-13Progressions that work — and whysongwriting

Goal: collect a working set of chord progressions and understand the logic underneath, so you can build your own instead of copying.

A progression is a sequence of chords. Some sequences feel inevitable and others feel arbitrary, and the difference is mostly two things: function (are you moving home → away → tension → home?) and root motion (how far the bass note travels).

Root motion has a clear pecking order. Down a fifth is the strongest — it's the circle of fifths from lesson 09, and it's what V→I does. Down a third is smooth and gentle, because the two chords share two notes. Up a second is bright and forceful, because they share nothing. Most memorable progressions are just these three moves in different orders.

L-14Cadences — how phrases endpunctuation

Goal: control the sense of finality. Cadences are the full stops, commas and question marks of music.

A cadence is the chord pair that closes a phrase, and it determines how finished the phrase sounds. There are four you need, ordered here from most conclusive to least.

  • Perfect authentic · V → I — full stop. Both chords in root position, melody landing on the tonic. Maximum closure, which is why almost every classical piece ends this way.
  • Plagal · IV → I — the "amen" cadence. Conclusive but soft and warm, because there's no leading tone doing the work.
  • Half · anything → V — a question mark. The phrase stops on tension and leaves you hanging, which makes it perfect for ending a verse that needs a chorus.
  • Deceptive · V → vi — the swerve. Everything sets up a resolution to I, and you get the relative minor instead. Bittersweet, and the single most effective way to extend a phrase that felt like it was ending.

The related trick worth having is the suspension: replace a chord's third with the fourth above the root, creating a mild dissonance, then let it fall to the third. That's a sus4 chord, and the resolution is so satisfying that it props up a huge amount of rock and gospel.

The four cadences, each preceded by the same setup

Play the authentic and the deceptive back to back. Identical until the final chord, and the deceptive one physically feels like a step missed on a staircase. That feeling is a musical device you now own.

Inversions weaken a cadence, which sounds like a downside and isn't. Weak cadences are how you end a phrase without ending the song — you need them mid-verse far more often than you need full stops.

Theme 4 · Lessons 15–18

Line and voice — making harmony move well

Knowing which chords to use is half the job. The other half is how you get from one to the next, which colours you add, and what melody rides on top. This is where correct starts becoming good.

L-15Voice leading — the smoothest path between chordsthe pro move

Goal: make the same progression sound dramatically better by changing nothing but the voicings.

Imagine each note of a chord as a separate singer holding a line. Voice leading is the craft of moving those singers from one chord to the next. The guiding principle is almost embarrassingly simple: move each voice as little as possible. Keep any note shared between the two chords exactly where it is (a common tone), and move everything else to the nearest available note.

Playing every chord in root position — the default when you're learning — forces all three voices to leap in parallel, which sounds blocky and amateurish. Using inversions to minimise motion produces a progression that seems to flow rather than lurch, and it's usually the single biggest improvement available to a beginner arrangement.

Two refinements. Contrary motion — bass moving down while the upper voices move up, or vice versa — sounds stronger and more independent than everything moving the same way. And the classical prohibition on parallel fifths and octaves exists because when two voices move in lockstep at those intervals they fuse into one and you lose a voice; you don't have to obey it, but you should know it's why some voicings sound thin.

The same progression, twice

Identical harmony. In the second version no voice moves more than two semitones and the top note barely budges — so the ear hears one continuous texture changing colour, rather than four separate blocks. Play them back to back a few times; this difference is worth internalising.

The parallel example is a legitimate effect — it's how a lot of film music and shoegaze works — but notice it sounds like one thick sound sliding rather than three independent parts. That's exactly what the old rule was warning about, stated as a fact rather than a prohibition.

L-16Extended and altered chords — the colours above the seventhspice

Goal: keep stacking thirds past the seventh, and learn to bend the results deliberately.

Stack one more third past the seventh and you get the ninth; again for the eleventh; again for the thirteenth. Past that you'd be back at the root, so thirteen is the ceiling. These are extensions, and they add colour without changing a chord's function — a Cmaj9 does everything a C major triad does, it just does it with more atmosphere.

A practical rule, since five- and six-note chords go muddy fast: you can leave notes out. The root and fifth are the most expendable — a bass player or the bass register handles the root, and the fifth adds little. The third and seventh are what define the chord's identity and must stay. Jazz pianists routinely voice a thirteenth chord as four notes: third, seventh, and two extensions.

Then there are alterations: sharpening or flattening an extension to increase tension. These belong almost exclusively on dominant chords, because a dominant is already unstable and alterations make its pull toward home even more violent. A 7♭9 is menacing, a 7♯9 is the Hendrix chord, a 7♯11 floats, a 7♭13 is dark and cinematic.

L-17Borrowed chords and modal interchangethe good stuff

Goal: legally use notes from outside your key, which is where most of the emotionally striking moments in pop music come from.

Everything so far has been diatonic — chords built only from the current key. Modal interchange (or mode mixture) breaks that: you borrow a chord from the parallel key or mode, the one sharing your tonic. In C major you can reach into C minor, take one of its chords, and drop it into the progression. The tonic doesn't change, so you don't lose your bearings — but the colour shifts sharply for one bar, and that contrast is the entire effect.

The borrowings that earn their keep in a major key:

  • iv (minor four) — the most-used borrowed chord in all of pop. Replacing IV with iv at the end of a phrase is instantly wistful.
  • ♭VII — from Mixolydian. Rock's favourite non-diatonic chord; think of any classic rock outro.
  • ♭VI — heroic and cinematic, especially moving to ♭VII then back to I.
  • ♭III — darkens things sharply. Common in metal and in soul.
  • ♭II (Neapolitan) — usually in first inversion just before the dominant. Dramatic and old-fashioned in the best way.

The reverse works too: a minor-key song can borrow the major IV or end on a major tonic — the Picardy third, a centuries-old trick for turning a dark piece hopeful in its final chord.

Borrowing from C minor while staying in C major

The I–iv–I example changes exactly one note from the diatonic version — A becomes A♭ — and it's the sound of a thousand bridge sections. Cheapest emotional upgrade in music.

L-18Melody — writing the part people actually rememberthe point of it all

Goal: turn a scale into a tune, using contour, rhythm, repetition and the relationship to the chords underneath.

A melody is not a random walk through a scale. It has four ingredients you can control independently.

Contour — the shape it draws in the air. Most memorable melodies have a simple arc: rise to a high point, then fall away. The highest note in a melody is a resource you spend once; put it at the emotional peak, not in bar two.

Rhythm — often more identifiable than the pitches. You can recognise most famous melodies from the rhythm alone, tapped on a table. A melody with an interesting rhythm and dull pitches beats the reverse every time.

Repetition — the mechanism by which anything becomes memorable. A short idea is a motif; repeat it at a different pitch and it becomes a sequence. Listeners need repetition far more than beginner writers assume; the instinct to keep introducing new material is almost always wrong.

Relationship to the harmony — the technical piece. Notes belonging to the current chord (chord tones) sound settled; notes from the scale that aren't in the chord (passing tones) sound like motion. The working rule: put chord tones on strong beats and passing tones on weak beats. Follow it and almost any note sequence works. Break it deliberately and you get tension you can resolve.

Making music — theory at the MicroFreak

Two lessons that put everything on the actual hardware: what the low end should do, and how four minutes of music gets built out of the sequencer's two slots.

L-19Basslines — the most important part nobody noticeslow end

Goal: write bass parts that define the harmony and the groove at once. This is the MicroFreak's natural home — one voice, low, punchy.

The bass has two jobs at once. Harmonically, the lowest note determines how a chord is heard — play C-E-G over an A and the ear hears A minor 7, not C major. The bass, not the chord, decides what the chord is. Rhythmically, the bass locks with the kick drum to create the groove; where it sits relative to the kick matters more than which notes it plays.

A ladder of bassline approaches, easiest first:

  • Root notes — one note per chord. Never wrong, and it's what most of pop does.
  • Root and fifth — alternate between them. Instant country, disco, or techno depending on the rhythm.
  • Octave jumps — root down, root up. The disco and Italo bassline.
  • Walking bass — a note per beat, moving mostly stepwise, chord tones on strong beats, passing tones connecting. Exactly lesson 18's rule applied to the bottom.
  • Pedal tone — hold one bass note while the chords change above it. Builds enormous tension and is trivially easy on a synth.

One practical warning that theory doesn't teach you but mixing does: the low end has no room. Two bass notes at once turns to mud, and a bassline that fights the kick drum turns to soup. Keep it monophonic, keep it in the same rhythmic pocket as the kick, and if it sounds muddy move the bass notes off the kick's beats rather than reaching for the filter.

L-20Form and arrangement — four minutes from two sequencesthe finish line

Goal: assemble everything into a piece with a shape, using the constraints the MicroFreak actually imposes.

Form is the large-scale plan: which sections happen in which order. Popular music mostly uses a handful — verse/chorus, AABA, or in electronic music a continuous build-and-release structure. The functions are the same regardless of genre: something establishes the material, something contrasts with it, something returns.

Contrast is the mechanism, and it works on more axes than just "different chords." You can contrast register (move the melody up an octave), density (drop to just bass and drums), harmonic rhythm (change chords twice as fast), timbre (open the filter, change the engine), or simply silence. A chorus doesn't feel like a chorus because it's louder; it feels like one because the verse before it held something back.

Now the hardware reality. The MicroFreak gives you two sequences per preset, A and B, plus four modulation lanes, plus Spice and Dice. That is not a limitation to work around — it maps almost exactly onto song form:

  • Sequence A = your verse material. Sequence B = your chorus or bridge. Switching A↔B in time is your arrangement.
  • Mod lanes = the build. Automate Cutoff opening across the loop and you have a riser without touching a fader.
  • Spice = the tension dial. Raise it through a section, and drop it hard at the moment B arrives.
  • Oct buttons = instant register contrast, which is the cheapest and most effective drop trick there is.

A concrete four-minute plan: A for 8 bars with the filter mostly closed → open Cutoff gradually over 8 more → raise Spice through 4 bars → switch to B with Oct up one and Spice back to zero → 16 bars of B → back to A with the filter closed for the outro. That's a complete piece of music, one preset, no overdubs.
